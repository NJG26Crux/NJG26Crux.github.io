History of 3D Printing

1945

The idea of 3D printing dates all the way back to 1945 when sci-fi writer Murray Leinster first described the technology with surprising accuracy in his short story “Things Pass By”. He envisioned a machine that could take his drawings and replicate them with a moving arm, using melted plastic to form 3D objects.

1971

Johannes F. Gottwald filing a patent for a Liquid Metal Extruder. He envisioned this extruder to be just like an office printer, only it would print 3D objects with metal rather than words with ink.

1974

David Jones 1974 article in which he perfectly describes the stereolithography process.

1981

Japanese inventor Dr. Hideo Kodama filed in 1981 the first prominent patent of the decade. He described his invention as a “rapid prototyping device”. More importantly, he was the first person to ever apply for a patent that described a laser beam curing process. Sadly, his patent never went through as he abandoned financing the patent one year after filing it.

1984

Jean-Claude André, Olivier de Witte, and Alain le Méhauté were colleagues working for French technology firm Alcatel and the French National Center for Scientific Research (CNRS) came forward with a patent in 1984. They followed Dr. Kodama’s focus of a “rapid prototyping device” to produce complex parts. Unfortunately, their employers at Alcatel and the CNRS weren’t interested in the technology and the trio was unable to fund their project after applying for the patent, forcing them to abandon the idea.

1984

The US Patent Office granted Bill Masters the first ever approved patent for any form of technology that now falls under the umbrella of 3D printing. Masters is also the inventor of the kayak after filling over 30 patents for its design! Masters ultimately abandoned his work on 3D printing (after producing a working machine in the early 1990s) to focus on his growing kayak business. [listen to a Pdcast of an interview of Bill HERE]

1984

Charles “Chuck” Hull, the father of 3D printing, filed a patent for a stereolithography system, only three weeks after the trio in France filed for theirs. His patent was approved in 1986 and he started his own company: 3D Systems. Two years later they released the groundbreaking first commercially available 3D printer – the SLA-1 – in 1988. Beyond the spectacular machine itself, Hull also developed the STL file format and digital slicing process that remain crucial to the 3D printing industry to this day.

1988

Selective laser sintering was first invented down in Texas by university student Carl Deckard (Dr. Carl Deckard, along with Dr. Joseph Beaman). Only an undergrad at the time, his printer was rudimentary and could only produce basic pieces of plastic. Also filed in 1988 was the patent for a fused deposition modeling machine by Scott Crump, a co-founder of Stratasys. It took until 1992 for the patent to be granted but Stratasys was quick to grab the lead in 3D printing with their new FDM machines.

1993

MIT professor Emanuel Sachs introduced the term “3D printing” to the lexicon, and the industry formerly known as “rapid prototyping” never looked back.

2000

Zcorp introducing a multicolor 3D printer. They repurposed the inkjet printing technology common to household full-color printers, adapting it to print colorful 3D objects.

2004

Adrian Bowyer starting the RepRap movement. 3D printers to manufacture more 3D printers, thus creating self-replicating machines. Prusa Research founder Josef Průša. Reprap self-replicating machines have been heralded as the future, with some believing they could start a second industrial revolution, bring an end to global capitalism, and save the environment.

2006

Objet introduced the first commercially available desktop 3D printer.

2008

Thingiverse, was launched.

2009

SLA technology patent ends (the one Chuck Hull had filed to bring his SLA-1 printer to market). Stratasys’ patent for FDM technology also ended. Interestingly, although the patent expired, Stratasys still has a trademark on the term FDM, which forces some of their competitors to either make a deal with them to use the terminology that’s popular with consumers and media, or use the less common term “fused filament fabrication” (FFF). New DIY printer kits based on open source Thingiverse, Objet, and Reprap technology.

2011

Cornell University achieved a scientific breakthrough by introducing the first 3D food printer.

2012

Formlabs launched the first realistically affordable SLA printer for consumers, the Form 1, via a Kickstarter campaign.

2014

Expirations of SLS patents. "The technology of all three major forms of 3D printing is in the public domain."

2014

NASA took a printer into space and successfully used it on the International Space Station.

2015

Cellink announcing the first commercial sale of “bioink” that allows the 3D printing of body tissue.

2016

GE and HP introduced 3D printing divisions

2016

A Dublin lab announcing (Professor Daniel Kelly, whose team at the Advanced Materials and Bioengineering Research Centre [AMBER] of Trinity College) that they could print human bone or cartilage.

2018

The first family moving into a fully 3D printed home.
